01 — Strategic Direction

The Design Thesis

The existing deck communicates something specific: Lemon Studios is not a quiet, prestige-first operation. It’s aggressive, contemporary, and unafraid of visual tension. The identity system codifies that energy into repeatable, scalable brand language.

Crosshatch Textures

The dark backgrounds in the deck use a fine crosshatch or linen weave — never flat black. This micro-texture adds depth and tactility, making screens feel like printed matter. It signals production craft without being decorative.

Highlighter Typography

Key phrases sit on bold color blocks — cyan, yellow, coral — like a designer’s markup on a proof. It’s the visual language of editing: flagging what matters, striking through what doesn’t. This makes the brand feel like it’s still in motion, still creating.

Split-Screen Composition

Nearly every slide uses a hard vertical divide — information on one side, image or giant typography on the other. This creates visual tension and forces the eye to choose. It’s confrontational in the best sense: it demands engagement.

Giant Watermark Type

Oversized, near-transparent section titles bleed off the edge of every composition. “FILMS,” “ORIGINALS,” “THEATER” — these function as architectural elements, not text. They anchor the layout and create a sense of scale that makes everything else feel cinematic.

Why These Three

Barlow Condensed is the display face — tall, compressed, industrial. Its condensed proportions let headlines go massive without eating the layout. The 900 weight in uppercase creates the same visual density as the deck’s section titles. Available in italic for softer moments (team bios, pull quotes).

Archivo handles body text — a grotesque that’s slightly warmer than Helvetica but just as disciplined. It reads cleanly at small sizes and has the weight range (300–900) to handle emphasis without switching families.

Space Mono brings the editorial edge. Used for labels, metadata, and navigation, its monospaced geometry creates a “production proof” feeling — like internal notes that weren’t meant for final print but look better than what was.

05 — Voice & Tone

How We Sound

Provocative

We say what others in the industry won’t. Not shock for shock’s sake — we provoke because we believe the status quo isn’t good enough. “If it’s never been done, you have our attention.”

“Every other production company will tell you they ‘elevate storytelling.’ We’ll tell you how many screens your story reached and what it earned.”

Direct

Short sentences. Active voice. No hiding behind passive construction or corporate jargon. Our copy reads like a conversation between peers, not a press release addressed to nobody.

“20 films. Five years. Three genres with proven commercial viability. That’s the fund.”

Grounded

Every bold claim is backed by a number, a name, or a result. We don’t deal in vague promises. If we say we’re the best at something, the receipt follows immediately.

When to Use Light Mode

Light mode is the exception, not the default. Use it when the context demands it: printed pitch decks that will be photocopied, formal investor correspondence, co-branded materials where a partner’s guidelines require light backgrounds, and email templates where dark backgrounds cause rendering issues. In all digital-first contexts — presentations, social media, website, video — default to dark. The crosshatch texture is the brand’s home turf.

08 — Usage Guidelines

Do and Don’t

Do

Use the crosshatch texture on dark backgrounds. Flat black feels generic — the texture is what makes it Lemon.

Don’t

Use more than one highlight color per headline. Pick one — cyan, yellow, or coral — and commit. Multiple colors in one line reads as circus, not editorial.

Do

Let giant watermark type bleed off the edge. It should feel architectural — bigger than the container, bigger than the screen. Crop it aggressively.

Don’t

Put highlight blocks on body text. Highlighters are for headlines and pull quotes only. Body text in color blocks makes everything look like a warning label.

Do

Use B&W photography with color overlays. The deck mixes grayscale imagery with bold color shapes — this contrast between documentary realism and graphic energy is core to the look.

Don’t

Use all three accent colors at equal weight. Cyan leads (15%), yellow supports (10%), coral punctuates (5%). Flattening the hierarchy kills the tension that makes the palette work.

Do

Use Space Mono for all metadata, labels, and navigation. The monospaced font is the “production notes” layer of the design — it signals internal, expert, in-progress.

Don’t

Use Barlow Condensed at normal weight for headlines. The display face only works at 700 or heavier. Anything lighter collapses the condensed proportions and looks anemic.
