# Audience Testing: Measuring Connection & Impact

Guide to evaluating screenplay effectiveness through test reads and market analysis.

## The Test Reader Framework

**Purpose:** Get honest feedback from people who represent your target audience BEFORE production.

### Reader Types

**1. Target Demographic** (Primary)
- Age, gender, interests match intended audience
- Fresh eyes (no film industry bias)
- Honest feedback (not trying to be "helpful")

**2. Craft Readers** (Secondary)
- Writers, directors, development execs
- Technical feedback (structure, pacing, craft)
- Industry perspective (marketability, trends)

**3. Diversity Reading** (Essential)
- Readers from different backgrounds
- Catch blind spots, stereotypes, cultural insensitivity
- Ensure universal themes land across cultures

### How Many Readers?

**Minimum:** 5-10 readers per draft
- 3-5 target demo
- 2-3 craft readers
- 2-3 diversity checks

**Red Flag:** If 60%+ say same thing, it's real (not just opinion)

## Test Reader Questions

### Engagement Questions
Have reader answer BEFORE discussing:

**Emotional Response:**
- On scale 1-10, how engaged were you?
- Which scene affected you most? (emotion check)
- When did you check the page count? (boredom indicator)
- Would you recommend this to a friend?

**Character Connection:**
- Who did you root for?
- Which character felt most real?
- Did you care if protagonist succeeded?
- Any character you wanted MORE of?

**Story Clarity:**
- What's the story about in one sentence?
- What's the theme?
- Any confusing moments?
- What did you expect to happen? (tests setup/payoff)

**Marketability:**
- Would you pay to see this?
- What genre is it?
- Who's the audience?
- What would the poster look like?

### Specific Scene Questions

For KEY scenes (opening, midpoint, climax):

**Opening (Pages 1-10):**
- Did it hook you immediately?
- When did you decide to keep reading?
- Too slow or too fast?

**Midpoint (Page ~55):**
- Did anything surprising happen here?
- Did the story feel like it changed direction?

**Climax (Pages 90-100):**
- Was it satisfying?
- Did you see it coming?
- Emotional impact?

## Common Test Reader Feedback & Fixes

### "I didn't care about the main character"

**DIAGNOSIS:** Character lacks empathy or agency

**FIX:**
- Add save-the-cat moment (show them doing something good)
- Give them underdog status (we root for strugglers)
- Show vulnerability (let us see them hurt)
- Make them active (choosing, not  reacting)

### "It was confusing"

**DIAGNOSIS:** Unclear stakes, motivation, or plot logic

**FIX:**
- Clarify goal (what does protagonist want?)
- Establish stakes (what happens if they fail?)
- Simplify plot (combine storylines)
- Add breadcrumbs (plant information earlier)

### "It dragged in the middle"

**DIAGNOSIS:** Act 2 lacks propulsion

**FIX:**
- Add midpoint twist
- Raise stakes (make it personal)
- Cut redundant scenes
- Tighten dialogue

### "The ending felt flat"

**DIAGNOSIS:** Unsatisfying climax or resolution

**FIX:**
- Ensure protagonist makes active choice
- Physical AND emotional resolution
- Callback to opening (show transformation)
- Surprise with HOW, not WHAT

### "I've seen this before"

**DIAGNOSIS:** Generic concept or execution

**FIX:**
- Find fresh angle (unexpected setting, POV, tone)
- Subvert one genre expectation
- Deepen theme (what's it REALLY about?)
- Unique character voices

## Coverage: Industry Format

Professional readers write "coverage" - synopsis + evaluation:

### Coverage Template

**LOGLINE:** (1-2 sentences)

**SYNOPSIS:** (1 page summary)

**EVALUATION:**

**Premise:** ☐ Excellent ☐ Good ☐ Fair ☐ Pass
**Structure:** ☐ Excellent ☐ Good ☐ Fair ☐ Pass
**Dialogue:** ☐ Excellent ☐ Good ☐ Fair ☐ Pass
**Characters:** ☐ Excellent ☐ Good ☐ Fair ☐ Pass

**RECOMMEND:** ☐ Consider ☐ Pass

**COMMENTS:** (Strengths, weaknesses, suggestions)

**Get 2-3 professional coverage reports** before submitting to agents/producers.

## Comp Analysis: Market Research

**Comps** = Comparable films that prove market for your screenplay

### Finding Good Comps

**Criteria:**
1. Similar genre/tone
2. Released in last 5-10 years (proves current market)
3. Successful (box office OR critical acclaim)
4. NOT mega-franchises (unfair comparison)

**Example - Psychological Thriller about Memory:**

GOOD COMPS:
- *Shutter Island* (2010) - $295M box office
- *Memento* (2000) - Cult classic, $40M on $9M budget
- *The Machinist* (2004) - Critical darling, actor showcase

BAD COMPS:
- *Inception* (2010) - Too big, Christopher Nolan franchise
- *Total Recall* (1990) - Too old, different era

### Using Comps for Development

**Ask:**
- What did these films do well? (Learn from success)
- What did audiences love? (Genre expectations)
- What was criticized? (Avoid their mistakes)
- Budget ballpark? (Production reality check)

**Positioning Statement:**
"It's *Shutter Island* meets *Arrival* - psychological thriller with sci-fi twist"

## Focus Group Testing (If Budget Allows)

**When:** Late drafts, before production

**Setup:**
- 10-15 target demographic readers
- Professional moderator
- 2-hour session

**Process:**
1. Read script (or watch proof-of-concept scene)
2. Individual written response (prevent groupthink)
3. Facilitated discussion
4. Specific scene reactions

**Cost:** $2K-$5K (but can save millions in bad production decisions)

## Red Flags from Test Readers

**STOP and Rewrite if:**

- 70%+ don't understand the plot
- Majority don't care about protagonist
- Nobody can identify the theme
- Genre confusion ("Is this comedy or drama?")
- All readers confused by same scene/moment
- Unanimous "the ending didn't work"

**ONE bad review = outlier**
**MANY saying same thing = real problem**

## The Rewrite Decision Tree

After test reads, ask:

**Minor Tweaks?** (70%+ positive, specific fixes needed)
- Polish dialogue
- Clarify scene or two
- Tighten pacing

**→ One rewrite pass, test again**

**Major Issues?** (50-70% positive, structural problems)
- Reorder scenes
- Combine/cut characters
- Rewrite Act 2

**→ Significant rewrite (4-8 weeks), test again**

**Fundamental Flaws?** (<50% positive, concept/character issues)
- Rethink premise
- Consider starting over
- OR shelve and move to next project

**→ Brutal honesty: Sometimes scripts don't work. Learn and move on.**

## Success Metrics

**Know your script is ready when:**

- ✅ 80%+ readers engaged throughout
- ✅ Majority correctly identify theme
- ✅ Clear consensus on genre
- ✅ Strong emotional reactions to key scenes
- ✅ Readers quote favorite lines
- ✅ "I'd pay to see this" responses
- ✅ Coverage recommends "CONSIDER"

**Until then:** Keep iterating.

## Final Testing Wisdom

**William Goldman:** "Nobody knows anything" (in Hollywood)

**BUT:** Patterns in feedback reveal truth. Listen for:
- What MULTIPLE people say
- WHERE they got bored/confused (page numbers align?)
- WHAT they remember days later (your memorable moments)

**Your Job:** Filter feedback through your vision. Not every note is right, but every pattern is significant.

**Remember:** Testing doesn't make the script less "yours" - it makes it connect better with who it's FOR.
