## 1. Adaptation Philosophy

This system applies **Shawn Coyne's Story Grid adapted for screen** — his structural spine (Foolscap, Five Commandments, Six Core Questions, Conventions vs. Obligatory Scenes) with working-screenwriter vocabulary:

- Coyne's **"All Is Lost"** is named the **Mid-Act Climax** in screenwriter parlance. They are synonymous.
- The **Turning Point** is the trigger that produces the Crisis question — **not** a sixth Commandment.
- The **TV-format altitudes** (pilot / episode / season / series) and the **Series Engine** taxonomy are borrowed industry concepts, integrated honestly with Coyne's spine — and labeled as such wherever they appear.
- **Comedy** is treated as a full External Content Genre (value spectrum: Laughter/Humiliation) that **always pairs with a second content genre** for the external value spine. It has its own conventions, obligatory scenes, six subgenres, and non-negotiable craft rules.

## 2. The Structural Spine

### 2.1 The Five-Leaf Clover

A framework for classifying your story across five genre dimensions. Pick all five and you've told us what kind of story you're writing, who's in it, and how it moves.

| Leaf | Question | Options |
|---|---|---|
| **Time** | How long? | Short (< 40 min) · Medium (40–60 min, TV episode) · Long (90–180 min, feature) |
| **Reality** | How real? | Factualism (true events) · Realism (could happen) · Fantasy (suspension of disbelief) · Absurdism (deliberately unreal) |
| **Style** | What tone? | Free text: Drama, Comedy, Thriller, Dark Comedy… |
| **Structure** | What plot pattern? | Arch-Plot (single hero, linear causality, closed ending — most commercial) · Mini-Plot (multiple heroes, internal focus, open ending — art house) · Anti-Plot (deliberately breaks rules — experimental) |
| **Content** | What's at stake? | **External** genre (conscious desire) + **Internal** genre (subconscious need) |

**External Content Genre** = the SURFACE story — what the audience sees the hero chase (save the world, catch the killer, get the girl). This drives the plot.

**Internal Content Genre** = the HIDDEN story — the change happening inside the hero. They don't know it yet, but this is what the story is REALLY about. This drives the character arc.

### 2.2 The Six Core Questions (Foolscap)

Your entire story on one page. If you can't fill this in, you don't yet have a story.

1. **External Genre** — surface genre in one line (e.g., "Action (Long, Realism, Arch-Plot)")
2. **Internal Genre** — the hero's inner change in one line (e.g., "Worldview – Maturation")
3. **Point of View** — first person, third limited, third omniscient
4. **Conscious Object of Desire** — what the hero THINKS they want (visible external goal)
5. **Subconscious Object of Desire** — what the hero actually NEEDS (invisible inner change; usually unrecognized until late)
6. **Controlling Idea / Theme** — the meaning in one sentence. Format: *"[Value] prevails when [cause]."* Example: "Love conquers all when we're willing to sacrifice."

Plus the **Core Value** (the value spectrum at stake — Life/Death, Love/Hate…) and the **Core Event** (the single most important scene the genre requires — the Showdown, the Proof of Love, the J'accuse).

### 2.3 Global Story Structure — the 25/50/25 Rule

| Act | Share | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Beginning Hook** | first 25% | Ordinary world → Inciting Incident disrupts balance → hero commits and crosses the threshold |
| **Middle Build** | middle 50% | Progressive complications escalate; hero's first plan fails; midpoint reversal; ends (~75% mark) at the **All Is Lost / Mid-Act Climax**, where the value charge is at its lowest and the Crisis question is posed at maximum heat |
| **Ending Payoff** | final 25% | Hero makes the crisis decision, takes climactic action, new equilibrium arrives. The theme lands here. Payoff for all setups. |

### 2.4 The Five Commandments of Storytelling

Every scene — and the whole story — needs five things. If any are missing, the scene feels flat.

1. **Inciting Incident** — the event that disrupts the status quo and demands a response. Global level: the story trigger. Scene level: the moment that starts the scene conflict.
2. **Progressive Complications** — obstacles and setbacks that escalate (at least 2–3), raising stakes and preventing easy resolution. Turning-point progressions reveal information (revelation) or change circumstances (action).
3. **Crisis** — the impossible choice forced on the protagonist. Presented as **Best Bad Choice** (both options bad) or **Irreconcilable Goods** (both options good, mutually exclusive). This is a **QUESTION**, not an action.
4. **Climax** — the protagonist's ANSWER to the crisis question — the decision or action taken. The value charge shifts most dramatically here.
5. **Resolution** — the new status quo after the climactic action. Answers: "What happens now?"

**The Strategy-Failure Precondition (Coyne's rule for Crisis):** the protagonist's INITIAL strategy for getting what they want must visibly, irreversibly fail *before* the Crisis question can be posed. If your "Crisis" still leaves the original plan intact, you have a Progressive Complication, not a Crisis. The failure of strategy is what creates the impossible choice.

**The Five Commandments fire at every altitude** — scene, sequence, act, episode, season, series. They just compress or expand.

### 2.5 Value Charge & Turning Points

- **Value Charge** — the emotional state of the core value at the beginning and end of a scene (e.g., Safe (+) → Threatened (−)). **Every scene must turn.** Flat scenes (no change) get cut.
- **Turning Point** — the moment the value charge flips. Two types: **Action** (circumstances change through physical action) or **Revelation** (information revealed changes understanding). The best scenes have both.
- **Value Progression** — the four-point emotional journey of the core value (always 4 points, never 5): **Positive → Positive/Negative → Negative → Negative-Negative** (or the reverse). Example for Action: Life → Wounded → Death → Damnation.

### 2.6 Conventions vs. Obligatory Scenes

- **Genre Conventions** — the recurring "furniture" the genre requires (a MacGuffin, a sidekick, a code of honor). Audiences subconsciously expect them; meeting them reassures the audience they're in good hands.
- **Obligatory Scenes** — the must-have STRUCTURAL moments the genre demands (the Showdown, the Proof of Love). If missing, the audience feels cheated. Every genre has 5–8.

## 3. External Content Genres

Eleven external content genres, each defined by a value spectrum:

| Genre | Value Spectrum | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Action | Life / Death | Four subgenres × four plot types (see §5) |
| Horror | Life / Damnation | |
| Crime | Justice / Injustice | |
| Thriller | Life/Damnation + Justice | Combines action, horror, crime |
| Western | Individual / Society | Frontier-noir spine |
| Eastern | Honor / Dishonor | Samurai, wuxia, frontier-bandit code — sibling of Western |
| War | Victory / Defeat | |
| Society | Power / Tyranny | Impotence → Subordination → Power → Tyranny; Core Event is The Revolution |
| Love | Love / Hate | |
| Performance | Respect / Shame | |
| **Comedy** | **Laughter / Humiliation** | **Always pairs with a second content genre** (see §6) |

### 3.1 Action

**Conventions**
- A MacGuffin (object of desire)
- A clearly defined villain/antagonist
- A speech in praise of the villain
- Hero sidekick or mentor
- The hero must be outmatched and have a personal stake
- Physical obstacles and escalating danger
- All is Lost moment where hero seems defeated
- Hero at the mercy of the villain scene

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Hero at the Mercy of the Villain | The villain has the upper hand and could destroy the hero | End of Middle Build (All is Lost) |
| Speech in Praise of the Villain | Someone explains why the villain is so formidable or why their plan makes sense | Early Middle Build |
| Hero Sidekick Helps | The sidekick provides critical support, information, or rescue | Middle Build or Climax |
| MacGuffin Revelation | The true nature or importance of the MacGuffin is revealed | Middle Build |
| Hero Gains Object of Desire | Hero achieves external goal (but may not keep it) | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

**Value Progression:** Life/Victory → Wounded/Compromised → Death/Defeat → Damnation/Fate Worse Than Death

### 3.2 Horror

**Conventions**
- Supernatural or threatening monster/force
- Enclosed space or isolated setting
- Mood of dread and suspense
- Protagonist with a fatal flaw or weakness
- The threat is incomprehensible or unexplainable
- Victims picked off one by one
- False ending or deceptive safety
- The monster/horror is vanquished (but may return)

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Monster Attack | First encounter with the threat — often kills someone | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| Hero Denies Threat | Protagonist refuses to believe the danger is real | Beginning Hook to Early Middle Build |
| Victims Picked Off | Secondary characters are eliminated one by one | Throughout Middle Build |
| False Ending | The threat appears defeated, but returns | Late Middle Build |
| Final Confrontation | Hero faces the monster/threat for the last time | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

**Value Progression:** Life/Safety → Threatened/Unconscious → Death → Damnation/Undead/Eternal Torment

### 3.3 Crime

**Conventions**
- MacGuffin (evidence, case, justice)
- Investigator protagonist (detective, cop, lawyer)
- Criminal antagonist with compelling motive
- Red herrings and false leads
- Clues revealed progressively
- Investigation methods (forensics, interrogation)
- Betrayal or inside threat
- Justice system or legal framework

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery of the Crime | The crime is discovered and investigation begins | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| Initial Investigation | Hero gathers first clues and suspects | Beginning Hook to Early Middle Build |
| Red Herring Reveal | False lead exposed, redirecting investigation | Middle Build |
| Reversal — New Evidence | Major clue changes understanding of the case | Late Middle Build |
| Criminal Exposed | The true perpetrator is revealed and confronted | Ending Payoff (Climax) |
| Justice Served | Criminal faces consequences (prison, death, or escape) | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

**Value Progression:** Justice/Order → Uncertainty/Gray Area → Injustice/Chaos → Tyranny/Lawlessness

### 3.4 Thriller

**Conventions**
- Ticking clock or deadline
- Life-and-death stakes
- Conspiracy or hidden threat
- Paranoia and distrust
- Information asymmetry (hero knows less than villain)
- Pursuit and evasion
- Seemingly impossible odds
- Hero must act alone or be isolated

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Inciting Threat | The danger is introduced (bomb, conspiracy, chase begins) | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| Clock Starts Ticking | Deadline or time limit is established | Beginning Hook |
| False Ally Betrayal | Someone hero trusted reveals their true allegiance | Middle Build |
| Hero Isolated | Support systems removed, hero must act alone | Late Middle Build |
| Race Against Time | Final race to stop the threat before deadline | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

**Value Progression:** Life/Freedom → Uncertainty/Paranoia → Death/Imprisonment → Damnation/Total Control

### 3.5 Western

**Conventions**
- Frontier or lawless setting
- Code of honor or personal justice
- Individual vs. corrupt society/system
- Use of violence to restore order
- Outsider or stranger arrives
- Community in need of protection
- Moral ambiguity of violence
- The lone hero who must leave or integrate

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Stranger Arrives | Outsider hero enters the community | Beginning Hook |
| Community Under Threat | The villain/threat to the town is established | Beginning Hook to Early Middle Build |
| Hero Refuses the Call | Protagonist initially wants to avoid involvement | Early Middle Build |
| Personal Stake Established | Something makes it personal for the hero | Middle Build |
| The Showdown | Final confrontation (often a duel or shootout) | Ending Payoff (Climax) |
| Hero Departs or Integrates | Hero either leaves town or becomes part of community | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

**Value Progression:** Civilization/Order → Moral Ambiguity → Lawlessness/Chaos → Savagery/Anarchy

### 3.6 Eastern

**Conventions**
- Honor-bound warrior code (bushido, wuxia jianghu, frontier-bandit creed)
- Master / disciple or sworn-brotherhood bond
- A weapon as extension of self (sword, spear, fists)
- Sworn debt or unfinished obligation drives the hero
- Stylized duel as moral test
- Corrupt authority versus righteous outlaw
- Discipline / mastery proven through ordeal
- Hero accepts personal cost (exile, death, anonymity) for the code

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Code Established | The honor code (bushido, wuxia oath, vendetta) that binds the hero is introduced | Beginning Hook |
| The Unforgivable Insult | The wrong, dishonor, or debt that demands a reckoning | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| Training / Mastery | Hero hones the skill or steels the will required to keep the code | Middle Build |
| Hero at the Mercy of a Greater Master | A defeat or lesson by a superior fighter exposes the hero's remaining flaw | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost / Mid-Act Climax) |
| The Duel | Stylized one-on-one confrontation that resolves the honor question | Ending Payoff (Climax) |
| Honor Restored or Forfeit | Hero pays the personal cost the code demands — exile, death, anonymity, or peace | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

**Value Progression:** Honor/Mastery → Compromised Honor → Dishonor/Disgrace → Damnation of the Name (legacy destroyed, code broken)

### 3.7 War

**Conventions**
- Military organization or unit
- Clear enemy or opposing force
- Mission or strategic objective
- Camaraderie and brotherhood
- Sacrifice for greater good
- Hierarchy and chain of command
- Survivor's guilt or PTSD
- Futility or cost of war theme

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Call to Service | Characters enlisted, drafted, or mobilized | Beginning Hook |
| Unit Formation | The military unit/team comes together | Beginning Hook to Early Middle Build |
| First Combat | Unit experiences their first battle or mission | Early to Mid Middle Build |
| Casualty or Loss | A beloved character dies or is wounded | Middle Build |
| Mission Impossible | Unit given seemingly unwinnable objective | Late Middle Build |
| Final Battle | Climactic combat sequence | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

**Value Progression:** Victory/Freedom → Stalemate/Pyrrhic Victory → Defeat/Occupation → Annihilation/Genocide

### 3.8 Society

**Conventions**
- A clearly drawn power structure (regime, family dynasty, institution)
- A ruling figure or class versus the powerless
- A protagonist who begins in impotence and is forced to act
- Subordination rituals — what the powerless must do to survive
- A tipping-point event where the powerless seize agency
- A speech, manifesto, or symbolic act that names the injustice
- The threat of tyranny if power is not contested
- A Revolution (or its suppression) as the genre Core Event

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Power Structure Established | The ruling order and the protagonist's position of impotence within it are drawn | Beginning Hook |
| The Unbearable Subordination | A specific act of oppression or humiliation makes the status quo intolerable | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| First Act of Defiance | The previously powerless protagonist commits the first irreversible act against the order | Early Middle Build |
| The Speech / Manifesto | The injustice is named publicly — argument is made for change | Middle Build |
| Power Tasted, Tyranny Risked | Protagonist gains real power and is tempted to use it the way the old order did | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost / Mid-Act Climax) |
| The Revolution (Core Event) | The old order is overthrown — or the protagonist is crushed by it | Ending Payoff (Climax) |
| New Order or Restored Tyranny | The new equilibrium reveals whether power was transformed or merely transferred | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

**Value Progression (canonical Coyne):** Power (agency, voice) → Subordination (constrained, surviving inside the order) → Impotence (no voice, crushed) → Tyranny (the revolution becomes the new oppression)

### 3.9 Love

**Conventions**
- Two protagonists (lovers)
- Initial attraction or meet-cute
- External obstacle to relationship
- Internal flaw preventing connection
- The "wrong" partner or love triangle
- Sacrifice for love
- Public declaration of love
- Commitment or partnership formation

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Meet Cute | The two lovers meet for the first time in a memorable way | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| First Attraction | Mutual recognition of attraction or chemistry | Beginning Hook |
| First Kiss or Intimacy | Physical expression of connection | Early to Mid Middle Build |
| The Break-Up or Separation | Lovers separated by external force or internal conflict | Late Middle Build (All is Lost) |
| Lovers Sacrifice | One lover sacrifices something important for the other | Late Middle Build to Climax |
| Proof of Love | Public declaration or undeniable demonstration of love | Ending Payoff (Climax) |
| Lovers Unite | Couple commits to relationship (marriage, partnership, etc.) | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

**Value Progression:** Love/Connection → Like/Attraction → Indifference/Separation → Hate/Betrayal

### 3.10 Performance

**Conventions**
- Public arena or stage
- Audience or judges
- Rivals or competitors
- Mentor or coach
- Skill progression and training
- Performance anxiety or stage fright
- Personal vs. public identity
- The big performance or competition

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery of Talent | Hero's natural ability or passion is revealed | Beginning Hook |
| Meeting the Mentor | Expert coach or teacher takes hero under wing | Beginning Hook to Early Middle Build |
| Training Montage | Hero develops skills through practice | Early to Mid Middle Build |
| First Public Performance | Hero performs before audience for first time | Middle Build |
| Performance Disaster | Major failure or humiliation on stage | Late Middle Build (All is Lost) |
| The Big Performance | Final competition, audition, or show | Ending Payoff (Climax) |
| Recognition or Award | Hero receives acknowledgment (applause, trophy, contract) | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

**Value Progression:** Respect/Mastery → Recognition/Competence → Shame/Incompetence → Humiliation/Complete Failure

## 4. Internal Content Genres

The subconscious need of the protagonist — the arc beneath the plot. Three families, eleven genres.

### Worldview

| Genre | Core Value | Description | Arc |
|---|---|---|---|
| Education | Naiveté → Sophistication | Character learns new information that changes their understanding of the world | From ignorance to knowledge through education/experience |
| Maturation | Immaturity → Maturity | Character grows from psychological immaturity to maturity | From selfishness to selflessness, from dependence to independence |
| Revelation | Blindness → Sight | Character experiences epiphany that transforms worldview | From delusion to truth through shocking revelation |
| Disillusionment | Idealism → Cynicism | Character's idealistic worldview is shattered by harsh reality | From innocence to experience, often tragically |

### Morality

| Genre | Core Value | Description | Arc |
|---|---|---|---|
| Punitive | Immorality → Punishment | Character makes immoral choice and suffers consequences | From transgression to justice/retribution |
| Redemption | Sin → Redemption | Character atones for past wrongs and finds redemption | From guilt to forgiveness through sacrifice |
| Testing | Integrity → Compromise or Integrity Maintained | Character's moral code is tested under extreme pressure | Moral dilemma that reveals true character |

### Status

| Genre | Core Value | Description | Arc |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pathetic | Powerlessness → Greater Powerlessness | Character tries to gain status but fails tragically | Downward spiral, elicits pity |
| Sentimental | Low Status → High Status (Unearned) | Character of low status is elevated through external fortune | Rags to riches through luck, not merit |
| Tragic | High Status → Low Status (Deserved) | Character of high status falls due to fatal flaw | Classic tragedy, hubris leads to downfall |
| Admiration | Low Status → High Status (Earned) | Character earns respect and status through merit and struggle | Overcomes odds through skill, courage, or perseverance |

## 5. Action Deep Dive

Coyne adapted for screen: Action breaks into **four subgenres**, each with **four plot types** (16 total). Choosing both sharpens convention and obligatory-scene work.

### 5.1 The Four Subgenres

| Subgenre | Description | Core Tension | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Adventure** | Hero traverses a hostile space toward a goal. The journey is the obstacle. | Hero vs. environment / unknown | Raiders of the Lost Ark, Mad Max: Fury Road, The Mummy, Apocalypto |
| **Duel** | Hero vs. a single equal-or-greater antagonist. The personal vendetta is the engine. | Hero vs. matched antagonist | Die Hard, Heat, The Bourne Identity, John Wick |
| **Epic** | Hero leads, joins, or saves a collective. Stakes scale beyond the individual. | Hero + collective vs. systemic threat | Gladiator, Braveheart, The Lord of the Rings, The Magnificent Seven |
| **Clock** | A hard deadline structures the entire story. Time is the antagonist. | Hero vs. countdown | Speed, Crank, 12 Strong, Phone Booth |

### 5.2 The Sixteen Plot Types

**Adventure**
- **Labyrinth** — Hero must navigate a confined, hostile maze (literal or social) to escape or recover something.
- **Monster** — Hero crosses a wilderness to confront or escape a non-human threat.
- **Environment** — The environment itself (storm, jungle, mountain, ocean) is the primary antagonist.
- **Doomsday** — Hero must reach a place or object before catastrophic, terminal stakes resolve.

**Duel**
- **Revenge** — Hero hunts a specific antagonist to settle a prior wrong.
- **Hunted** — Hero is the prey; survival depends on outwitting an equal predator.
- **Machiavellian** — Hero and antagonist are matched strategists; the duel is psychological/tactical.
- **Collision** — Two near-mirror antagonists on a converging path; one must break before the other.

**Epic**
- **Rebellion** — Hero leads (or joins) an uprising against a dominant system.
- **Conspiracy** — Hero unravels a hidden, distributed antagonist that threatens a collective.
- **Vigilante** — Hero protects a community when institutions cannot or will not.
- **Savior** — Hero is the only one who can lead the collective through an existential threat.

**Clock**
- **Ransom** — Innocent lives held; deadline + demand drive the structure.
- **Countdown** — A device, event, or catastrophe will resolve at a fixed time unless stopped.
- **Holdout** — Hero must survive in place until relief arrives or the threat exhausts itself.
- **Fate** — A personal countdown (terminal illness, sentence, deadline) forces the hero's final acts.

### 5.3 Subgenre-Specific Obligatory Scenes

**Adventure**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Crossing the Threshold | Hero leaves the known world for the hostile space | Beginning Hook |
| First Encounter with the Space | The environment proves its lethal nature | Early Middle Build |
| The Map / Guide Fails | The plan collapses; hero must improvise or perish | Mid-Act Climax |
| The Recovery | Hero secures (or loses) the object of the quest | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

**Duel**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Worthy Adversary Established | The antagonist is shown to be the hero's equal or superior | Beginning Hook |
| First Direct Engagement | Hero and antagonist meet — neither wins decisively | Early Middle Build |
| Hero at the Mercy of the Antagonist | Antagonist has the upper hand; hero's strategy fails | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost / Mid-Act Climax) |
| Final Confrontation | Hero vs. antagonist, no retreat possible | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

**Epic**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Call to Lead | Hero is asked, forced, or volunteers to lead the collective | Beginning Hook |
| Speech in Praise of the Villain | Audience learns why the systemic threat is so formidable | Early Middle Build |
| A Cost to the Collective | Allies fall; the price of the cause is paid in flesh | Middle Build / Mid-Act Climax |
| The Final Stand | The collective acts as one against the systemic threat | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

**Clock**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Clock Starts | The deadline is established with specific terms | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| False Time Gained | A delay or extension lets the hero think they have room — they don't | Middle Build |
| Time Runs Out, Plan Fails | The deadline arrives and the strategy hasn't worked | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost / Mid-Act Climax) |
| Race to the Deadline | Final dash to stop, deliver, or survive the countdown | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

## 6. Comedy Deep Dive

**Comedy is a full External Content Genre in this adaptation** — with one structural rule that governs everything:

> **Comedy always pairs with a second content genre.** Comedy carries the tone; the paired genre carries the external stakes (the value spine). A rom-com pairs Comedy + Love. A buddy action-comedy pairs Comedy + Action. When you work in Comedy, you must deliver **BOTH** sets of obligatory scenes — Comedy's and the paired genre's.

**Value Spectrum:** Laughter / Humiliation

**Value Progression:** Laughter/Joy/Connection → Amusement/Mild Chuckle → Boredom/Discomfort/Alienation → Humiliation/Despair/Comedy Weaponized

### 6.1 Comedy Genre Conventions

1. **The Comic Flaw** — protagonist has a specific, visible flaw that generates humor (vanity, cluelessness, rigidity)
2. **The Straight Man** — at least one character who sees reality clearly and reacts to the absurdity
3. **Escalation Engine** — situations get progressively worse/more absurd; comedy that stays flat dies
4. **Comic Set Pieces** — 2–3 extended sequences designed specifically for laughs (the trailer moments)
5. **The Ticking Embarrassment** — a social deadline, event, or revelation the protagonist is trying to avoid
6. **Mistaken Identity / Deception** — someone is pretending, hiding something, or operating under a misunderstanding
7. **The Confidant** — a friend/sidekick who knows the truth and either helps or begs the protagonist to stop
8. **The Authority Figure** — represents the rules, norms, or power structure the comedy subverts

### 6.2 Comedy Obligatory Scenes

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Comic Premise Established | The absurd situation, flaw, mistaken identity, or fish-out-of-water setup is revealed | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| First Big Laugh (Comic Set Piece #1) | First extended comedic sequence that demonstrates the comic engine — proves what the movie will be | Late Beginning Hook |
| The Lie Deepens / Complication Escalation | Protagonist doubles down instead of coming clean; what was manageable becomes unmanageable | Early to Mid Middle Build |
| The Serious Moment (Comedy Drops Away) | The mask falls, real emotion surfaces — pain, vulnerability, genuine connection. Earns the comedy its weight | Mid to Late Middle Build |
| Public Exposure / The Jig Is Up | The lie is exposed, the disguise fails, the truth comes out at the worst possible moment — usually publicly | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost) |
| The Big Sequence (Comic Set Piece #2) | The largest, most elaborate comedic sequence — the one people remember and quote | Late Middle Build to Early Ending Payoff |
| Growth Through Humor | Protagonist uses what they have learned to resolve the crisis — often with a final comedic twist that is both funny and emotionally true | Ending Payoff (Climax) |
| The New Normal (with a Laugh) | Order restored but changed. The final beat is a warm laugh — everything will be okay, and life is absurd | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

### 6.3 The Six Comedy Subgenres

#### Rom-Com (Romantic Comedy)
- **Description:** Comedy layered over a Love story. The comic engine runs on romantic misunderstanding, neurosis, and the gap between what the heart wants and what the mouth says.
- **Core Tension:** Can these two people get past their own neuroses to actually be together?
- **Typical Pairing:** Love (Love/Hate)
- **Examples:** When Harry Met Sally, The Big Sick, Crazy Rich Asians, 10 Things I Hate About You, Notting Hill

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Meet-Cute | Memorable, quirky, humorous first encounter between the two leads — sets the comic and romantic tone | Beginning Hook (Inciting Incident) |
| The Almost-Kiss / Chemistry Proof | A moment of undeniable chemistry where they almost connect — interrupted or deflected | Early Middle Build |
| The Misunderstanding Breakup | Communication breakdown or discovered deception drives the couple apart at their most vulnerable | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost) |
| The Grand Gesture | Extravagant, often public display to win the other back — the airport chase, the rain speech, the boom box | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

#### Buddy Comedy
- **Description:** Mismatched pair forced together. Humor from friction between opposite personalities. The bond forged under fire becomes the emotional spine.
- **Core Tension:** Can these mismatched people who cannot stand each other learn to work together?
- **Typical Pairing:** Love (platonic) or Status (Admiration); buddy action-comedies pair with Action
- **Examples:** The Hangover, Step Brothers, Rush Hour, The Heat, Lethal Weapon

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Forced Pairing | Awkward, antagonistic initial encounter — neither wants to be there | Beginning Hook |
| The "I Can't Work With This Person" Blow-Up | Clash of methods leads to spectacular failure or public argument | Early Middle Build |
| The Reluctant Respect Moment | One partner grudgingly admits the other has value — often after the other saves them | Mid Middle Build |
| The Separation / Betrayal | Partnership dissolves — differences become irreconcilable or trust is broken | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost) |
| The Reunion Under Fire | Reunite with integrated strengths to overcome the final obstacle together | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

#### Fish Out of Water
- **Description:** Protagonist dropped into a world where none of their rules apply. Comedy from culture clash, adaptation, and the outsider's mirror on the new world's absurdities.
- **Core Tension:** Can this person survive (and eventually thrive) where none of their rules apply?
- **Typical Pairing:** Performance (Respect/Shame) or Society (Power/Tyranny)
- **Examples:** Legally Blonde, Coming to America, Elf, Crocodile Dundee, The Proposal

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Arrival / First Encounter | Protagonist enters the new world — visual and tonal contrast established immediately | Beginning Hook |
| The Embarrassing Misunderstanding | Culture clash produces a spectacular social faux pas — comedy of errors | Early Middle Build |
| The Adaptation Montage | Protagonist begins learning the rules — partial success mixed with continued failure | Mid Middle Build |
| The Moment They Almost Belong | Protagonist is accepted — but their old identity resurfaces or is exposed | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost) |
| The Outsider's Gift | Protagonist's unique perspective from outside solves a problem insiders could not see | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

#### Satire / Dark Comedy
- **Description:** Comedy weaponized against institutions, hypocrisy, or taboo. Exaggeration exposes truth. May venture into dark territory — death, corruption, moral rot — treated with comic detachment.
- **Core Tension:** Can the absurdity of the system be exposed without destroying the protagonist?
- **Typical Pairing:** Society (Power/Tyranny) or Crime (Justice/Injustice)
- **Examples:** Dr. Strangelove, Thank You for Smoking, The Death of Stalin, Don't Look Up, Network

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The System Revealed | The institution, power structure, or hypocrisy that will be satirized is shown in full absurd glory | Beginning Hook |
| The Protagonist Plays Along | Hero initially benefits from or participates in the corrupt system — complicity before rebellion | Early Middle Build |
| The Line They Won't Cross | The system asks something the protagonist cannot stomach — the moral line | Mid to Late Middle Build |
| The Public Unmasking | The hypocrisy or absurdity of the system is exposed publicly — often with ironic consequences | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

#### Farce / Slapstick
- **Description:** Comedy at its most physical and mechanical. Multiple deceptions run simultaneously, doors open at the wrong time, chaos snowballs with algebraic precision. The lie is the motor.
- **Core Tension:** Can the protagonist maintain control as everything falls apart?
- **Typical Pairing:** Love or Performance
- **Examples:** Some Like It Hot, Airplane!, Clue, The Birdcage, Game Night

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Setup of Spinning Plates | Multiple deceptions, identities, or commitments are established — all must be maintained simultaneously | Beginning Hook |
| The Near-Miss | Two worlds almost collide — the protagonist barely avoids exposure through desperate improvisation | Early to Mid Middle Build |
| The Collision of All Lies | Every deception converges at once — all spinning plates crash in the same scene | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost) |
| The Total Chaos Sequence | The most elaborate physical/situational set piece — pure comedic pandemonium | Ending Payoff (Climax) |

#### Coming-of-Age Comedy
- **Description:** Comedy of growing up — first experiences, generational conflict, peer pressure, and innocence lost through laughter. The humiliation of adolescence treated as universal comedy.
- **Core Tension:** Can the protagonist survive the humiliation of growing up and emerge as a real person?
- **Typical Pairing:** Love or Performance
- **Examples:** Superbad, Lady Bird, Booksmart, Ferris Bueller's Day Off, American Graffiti

**Obligatory Scenes**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| The Plan to Be Cool | Protagonist hatches a scheme to gain status, get the girl/guy, or prove maturity — doomed from conception | Beginning Hook |
| The Party Disaster | The big social event goes spectacularly wrong — the plan backfires in public | Middle Build |
| The Adult Moment | Protagonist faces a genuinely adult situation they are not ready for — comedy gives way to real stakes | Late Middle Build (All Is Lost) |
| The Reconciliation | Protagonist reconciles with parents, mentors, or friends — accepts who they actually are vs. who they pretended to be | Ending Payoff (Resolution) |

### 6.4 Comedy Craft Rules — Non-Negotiable

1. **ACT 2 IS ESCALATION.** The engine of a comedy is a plan that keeps getting worse. Each attempt to fix things creates a bigger mess. Ask about the ESCALATION MECHANISM, not just the obstacle.
2. **THE SET PIECE IS THE SCENE YOU'D PUT IN THE TRAILER.** Not the emotional climax — the funniest moment. The bridal shower in Bridesmaids, the bunk beds in Step Brothers, the dinner party in Meet the Parents. Ask what THAT scene looks like.
3. **EVERY CHOICE SHOULD IMPLY A FUNNY SCENE.** Not "He learns a lesson" but "He gives a toast at the party that starts as charming and ends with him accidentally insulting everyone." The reader should SEE the comedy from the choice alone.
4. **RELATIONSHIP COMEDY > PLOT COMEDY.** The funniest movies are about the dynamic between two people, not about events happening TO one person. Ask about the RELATIONSHIP ENGINE.
5. **THE UNCOMFORTABLE CHOICE IS THE FUNNIEST.** The version the writer is afraid to commit to is usually the one audiences love most. Push toward maximum embarrassment, not maximum drama.
6. **REFERENCE THE COMPS.** If the treatment mentions specific movies (Step Brothers, Bridesmaids, etc.), use them: "In Bridesmaids, Annie's war with Helen escalates from passive-aggressive to physically destructive — where does YOUR war cross that line?"

### 6.5 Comedy Development ("Grill") Rules

When pressure-testing a comedy treatment:

- Push toward **ESCALATION and CRINGE**, not darkness and tragedy.
- "Uncomfortable" in a comedy = the scene that makes you laugh and cover your face simultaneously. Not psychological trauma.
- Choices should imply **FUNNY SCENES** the reader can picture immediately.
- Reference the treatment's comps if it mentions specific comedies.
- **The funniest version is usually the right version.** Push toward it.

## 7. Film vs. Television

The Foolscap operates at **altitude-appropriate scopes**. The same Five Commandments fire at every altitude — they just compress or expand. Default is `feature`.

### 7.1 Media Formats

| Format | Shape |
|---|---|
| **Feature** | Single closed Foolscap — 25% Hook / 50% Build / 25% Payoff at the global level |
| **TV Pilot** | Episode-scope Foolscap that ALSO plants the series mega-Foolscap Hook |
| **TV Episode** | Compressed Foolscap (Cold Open / Middle Acts / Climax + tag) |
| **TV Season** | Full Foolscap with episodes as Progressive Complications inside the Middle Build |
| **TV Series** | Mega-Foolscap with seasons as Progressive Complications |

### 7.2 The Series Engine *(borrowed industry concept — not Coyne canon)*

Showrunner vocabulary for the renewable mechanism that generates stories week after week. Integrated because no Story Grid analysis of a series holds without a repeatable Inciting Incident generator. **The Pilot's job is to DEMONSTRATE the engine running.**

| Engine | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Procedural** | Self-contained A-story per episode; status quo restored each week. Slow background drift. | Law & Order, NCIS, House M.D. |
| **Serialized** | Each episode is a chapter of the larger arc; status quo erodes irreversibly. | Breaking Bad, The Americans, Succession |
| **Hybrid** | Self-contained A-story plus serialized B/C threads; slow erosion under episode wins. | Better Call Saul, The Good Wife, The X-Files |
| **Anthology** | New engine each season (or each episode). No continuous protagonist or world. | Fargo, True Detective, Black Mirror |

### 7.3 TV Format Conventions

**TV Pilot**
- Demonstrate the Series Engine — show the audience what a representative episode looks like
- Plant the Series Question (the mega-Foolscap's Hook)
- Deliver the episode-level Climax that proves the show's External Genre Core Event in miniature
- Establish Central Relationships that will drive B-stories and Internal Genre arcs

**TV Episode**
- A-story resolves the Five Commandments inside the episode
- B-story is compressed and thematically rhymes with A
- C-story is a partial / serialized seed for future episodes
- Act breaks should land on Turning Points across multiple storylines simultaneously
- Tag / button (optional) gives a character grace note or button joke

**TV Season**
- Episodes 1–2 = Beginning Hook (premiere arc)
- Mid-season episodes = Middle Build (Mid-Act Climax around the mid-season finale, if applicable)
- Season finale = Ending Payoff (delivers the season Core Event AND opens the next Inciting Incident)
- Penultimate episode often poses the season Crisis at maximum heat

**TV Series (mega-Foolscap)**
- Pilot + Season 1 = series Beginning Hook
- Middle seasons = series Middle Build
- Final season = series Ending Payoff
- Series finale must deliver the External Content Genre's Core Event at full global stakes
- Series finale must also resolve the Subconscious Need / Internal Genre arc

### 7.4 TV-Specific Obligatory Scenes

**Pilot**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Engine Demonstrated | Audience sees the mechanism that will generate next week's story | Within Pilot |
| Series Question Planted | The mega-Foolscap Hook — the question the show is going to argue about | Within Pilot |
| Episode Core Event Delivered | The External Genre Core Event in miniature so audience knows the show's contract | Pilot Climax |
| Central Relationship Established | The human dynamic that will fuel B-stories is set up | Within Pilot |

**Episode**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| A-Story Resolved | Inside the episode, A-story closes its Five Commandments | Episode Climax |
| B-Story Advanced | B-story (often character / relationship / theme) moves a meaningful step | Episode arc |
| Serialized Seed | C-story or hook for next episode — earns the come-back | Late in episode / tag |

**Season**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Season Hook | Premiere arc raises the season Crisis question | Eps 1–2 |
| Mid-Season Mid-Act Climax | Major Turning Point that reframes the season Crisis | Mid-season finale or equivalent |
| Penultimate Problem | Season Crisis posed at maximum heat — the "Penultimate Problem" tradition | Episode before finale |
| Season Climax / Core Event | Season finale delivers the season-level External Core Event | Finale |
| Next-Season Inciting Incident | Finale plants the hook for the next season | Finale tag |

**Series**

| Scene | Description | Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Pilot Beginning Hook | Pilot + S1 ground the mega-Foolscap | Season 1 |
| Series Mid-Act Climax | Middle seasons deliver a global Turning Point | Middle seasons |
| Series Climax / Global Core Event | Final season delivers the External Genre Core Event at full stakes | Final season |
| Subconscious Need Resolved | Series finale closes the Internal Genre arc — the show's ultimate argument | Series finale |

## 8. Glossary

**All Is Lost / Mid-Act Climax** — Synonymous terms. Coyne calls it "All Is Lost"; screenwriters call it the "Mid-Act Climax." It is the Crisis of the Middle Build — the end of Act 2, roughly the 75% mark — NOT the midpoint. At this moment, the protagonist's strategy has fully failed, the value charge is at its lowest, and the Crisis question is posed at maximum heat. The Ending Payoff answers it.

**Beginning Hook (25%)** — The first quarter. Introduces the ordinary world, the Inciting Incident, and the quest; ends with the hero crossing the threshold into the extraordinary world.

**Climax** — The protagonist's ANSWER to the crisis question — the decision or action they take. The turning point where the value charge shifts most dramatically.

**Controlling Idea / Theme** — The meaning of the story in one sentence: "[Value] prevails when [cause]." What the audience should feel/understand after the climax.

**Conscious Object of Desire** — What the protagonist THINKS they want; the external goal that drives the plot.

**Conventions** — The expected narrative elements ("furniture") a genre requires. Meeting them reassures the audience they're in good hands.

**Core Event** — The single most important scene in the story — the moment the genre requires (the Showdown, the Proof of Love, the Revolution).

**Core Value** — The value spectrum at stake, expressed as positive/negative poles (Life/Death, Love/Hate, Laughter/Humiliation). Every scene should charge this value one way or the other.

**Crisis** — The impossible choice: Best Bad Choice or Irreconcilable Goods. A QUESTION, not an action. Precondition: the protagonist's initial strategy must fail first — otherwise it's a Progressive Complication.

**Ending Payoff (25%)** — The final quarter. Crisis decision, climactic action, new equilibrium. The controlling idea lands here.

**Five Commandments** — The five required elements of every scene and the global story: Inciting Incident, Progressive Complications, Crisis, Climax, Resolution.

**Five-Leaf Clover** — Classification across five genre dimensions: Time, Reality, Style, Structure, Content (External + Internal).

**Foolscap** — The whole story on one page: the Six Core Questions plus the three acts.

**Inciting Incident** — The event that disrupts the status quo and sets the story/scene in motion.

**Media Format** — The altitude at which the Foolscap operates: feature, TV pilot, episode, season, or series.

**Middle Build (50%)** — The middle half. Progressive complications, midpoint reversal, and the All Is Lost / Mid-Act Climax where the hero earns their transformation through struggle.

**Obligatory Scenes** — The must-have moments that define a genre; if missing, the audience feels cheated. Every genre has 5–8.

**Progressive Complications** — Escalating obstacles that raise stakes and force the protagonist toward the impossible choice.

**Resolution** — The new status quo after the climax; the consequences of the choice and how the world has changed.

**Series Engine** *(borrowed industry concept — not Coyne)* — The renewable mechanism that generates a show's stories week after week: Procedural, Serialized, Hybrid, or Anthology. The Pilot must demonstrate it running.

**Strategy Failure (Crisis Precondition)** — Coyne's rule: the protagonist's INITIAL strategy must visibly, irreversibly fail before the Crisis can arrive. If the plan is still intact, you have a Progressive Complication, not a Crisis.

**Subconscious Object of Desire** — What the protagonist ACTUALLY needs; the internal transformation required for fulfillment, often unrecognized until late.

**Turning Point** — The moment in a scene where the value charge changes: ACTION (circumstances change) or REVELATION (information changes understanding). The best scenes have both. The Turning Point is the trigger that produces the Crisis question — not a sixth Commandment.

**Value Charge** — The emotional state of the core value at the start and end of a scene (Safe (+) → Threatened (−)). Every scene must turn; flat scenes get cut.

**Value Progression** — The four-point journey of the core value (always 4 points, never 5): Positive → Positive/Negative → Negative → Negative-Negative. Example: Life → Wounded → Death → Damnation.
